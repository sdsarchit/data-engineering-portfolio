Key findings, mathematical methodologies, and model structures from the published NJIT paper.

Abstract & Problem Goal

Geomagnetic storms are primary space weather events that can cause severe damage to Earth's power grids, networking setups, and satellites. Measuring the **Disturbance Storm Time (DST)** index is crucial for quantifying geomagnetic storm size and intensity.

This research performs a rigorous **Feature Importance Analysis** of the *DST Transformer model*—a state-of-the-art neural architecture combining Multi-Head Attention and Bayesian Deep learning (Monte Carlo Dropout layers) to estimate prediction uncertainty.

XAI Methodology

  • Kernel SHAP: Generates Shapley values based on cooperative game theory to measure the average margin contribution of each solar feature across 1,098 NASA wind samples.
  • LIME Tabular Explainer: Approximates the black-box DST Transformer model locally around specific instances to explain why the model made a positive or negative forecast.
  • Attention Score Maps: Extracts raw weight vectors from the Multi-Head Attention layer to visualize step-by-step correlation grids.

Key Analytical Insights

- Scalar B (IMF Magnitude) is mathematically proven as the single most critical feature driving DST index forecasting.

- BZ GSE (Z-component Magnetic Field) exhibits a massive negative correlation coefficient of -0.9556 with Scalar B. When BZ GSE tilts negative (southward), it allows solar wind energy to transfer into Earth's magnetosphere, triggering geomagnetic storms.

- **SW Plasma Temperature** and **Plasma Speed** act as strong supporting indicators.

- **SW Proton Density**, **Flow pressure**, and **E electric field** contain near-zero SHAP impact scores, confirming they can be safely pruned to reduce model training complexity.
